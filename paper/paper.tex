\begin{abstract}
Discovering causal relationships requires controlled experiments, but experimentalists face a sequential decision problem: each intervention reveals information that should inform what to try next. Traditional approaches such as random sampling, greedy information maximization, and round-robin coverage treat each decision in isolation, unable to learn adaptive strategies from experience. We propose Active Causal Experimentalist (ACE), which learns experimental design as a sequential policy. Our key insight is that while absolute information gains diminish as knowledge accumulates---making value-based reinforcement learning unstable---\emph{relative} comparisons between candidate interventions remain meaningful throughout. ACE exploits this via Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), learning from pairwise intervention comparisons rather than non-stationary reward magnitudes. We formalize this advantage with a scale-invariance result (Proposition~\ref{prop:scale-invariance}) and validate it empirically: across 5-, 15-, and 30-node synthetic SCMs, coupled Duffing oscillators, and graph misspecification ablations, ACE achieves \PARTIAL{70--71\%}{a} improvement over passive and heuristic baselines, and \TBD{ace-vs-boed-pct} improvement over a Bayesian optimal experimental design baseline at equal intervention budgets. Notably, the learned policy autonomously discovers that collider mechanisms require concentrated interventions on parent variables, a theoretically-grounded strategy that emerges purely from experience.

\section{Introduction}
% =======================================================================

Every experimentalist faces limited resources to explore vast possibility spaces. Testing all pairwise combinations of 100 compounds requires 4{,}950 experiments; a 10-component alloy across 5 temperatures faces $5^{10}$ configurations. Modern simulation environments---from climate modeling to drug discovery---enable rapid experimentation but expose vast parametric spaces with hundreds of interacting variables. The goal is not merely prediction but causal understanding: identifying which parameters actually drive outcomes, distinguishing causal pathways from spurious correlations, and discovering intervention targets that generalize. Random exploration becomes hopelessly inefficient, while domain expertise may not scale to simulation complexity.

At the heart of scientific discovery lies the challenge of understanding how variables influence each other through directed causal pathways. Rather than passively observing correlations, experimentalists actively manipulate variables through interventions to isolate causal effects. The efficiency of this learning process depends critically on which variables to intervene upon and at what values to set them. While theoretical results establish bounds on the number of interventions required for causal identification \citep{eberhardt2005number,eberhardt2006n}, these worst-case guarantees provide limited practical guidance for the adaptive, sequential decision-making that characterizes real experimental campaigns.

Traditional approaches employ static heuristics---random, round-robin, greedy information maximization \citep{murphy2001active,hauser2012characterization}---that cannot transfer insights between systems, balance multi-faceted constraints, or adapt based on what has been learned. Recent Bayesian active causal discovery methods such as ABCI \citep{toth2022active} and CBED \citep{tigas2022interventions} maintain explicit posteriors over causal graphs, but pay substantial computational cost and address structure discovery rather than the complementary problem of mechanism estimation we study here.

We present \textbf{Active Causal Experimentalist (ACE)}, which learns experimental design strategies via sequential decision-making. ACE models the scientific process as an iterative cycle: propose interventions, update mechanism beliefs, adapt strategy. ACE learns from experimental outcomes via Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) \citep{rafailov2023direct}, using pairwise comparisons between candidate interventions to develop adaptive strategies. This preference-based approach avoids the need to estimate explicit value functions, a critical advantage given that the rewards from experiments are inherently non-stationary as knowledge accumulates.

\textbf{Contributions.} We make three concrete claims, each empirically validated against state-of-the-art baselines:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,topsep=0.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item \textbf{Empirical:} ACE outperforms a Bayesian optimal experimental design baseline---representative of \citep{toth2022active,tigas2022interventions}---by \TBD{ace-vs-boed-pct}\% on the 5-node benchmark with N=\PARTIAL{10}{p} seeds (Section~\ref{sec:results-main}, Table~\ref{tab:main-results}). Across larger 15- and 30-node systems and a graph misspecification ablation, ACE maintains its advantage with graceful degradation (Section~\ref{sec:results-scale}).
\item \textbf{Theoretical:} We formalize the scale-invariance of preference learning under diminishing reward magnitudes (Proposition~\ref{prop:scale-invariance}). Empirical validation shows reward magnitudes drop $\sim$500$\times$ over training while DPO ranking remains stable (Spearman $\rho > 0.85$).
\item \textbf{Emergent strategy:} Without explicit instruction, the learned policy concentrates 99.8\% of interventions on collider parents, matching the strategy causal theory identifies as optimal. This suggests preference learning can recover principled experimental strategies through experience.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
% =======================================================================
\label{sec:related}

Our work builds on four research threads: theoretical causal discovery, Bayesian active experimental design, learning-based approaches, and preference optimization.

\textbf{Causal discovery theory.} Learning causal structures is NP-hard \citep{chickering1996learning}. Eberhardt et al.\ showed $n-1$ interventions suffice for $n$-variable systems \citep{eberhardt2005number,eberhardt2006n}, but worst-case bounds provide limited guidance for adaptive design. Information-theoretic methods select interventions by expected gain \citep{murphy2001active}, with $(1-1/e)$ approximation guarantees \citep{shanmugam2015learning}. Adaptive methods reduce required interventions versus non-adaptive \citep{hauser2012characterization,cho2016reconstructing}.

\textbf{Bayesian active causal discovery.} ABCI \citep{toth2022active} jointly performs causal discovery and reasoning by maintaining a full posterior over causal graphs using Gaussian processes, designing experiments maximally informative about target queries. CBED \citep{tigas2022interventions} embeds Bayesian causal discovery within optimal experimental design to select both intervention targets and values, with demonstrations on the DREAM gene regulatory network. \citet{zhang2023bayesian} extends this to multi-fidelity settings using mutual-information acquisition. \citet{zhou2024sample} addresses sample efficiency via polynomial-time DAG sampling. These methods focus on \emph{structure discovery}---learning the causal graph itself. ACE addresses the complementary problem of \emph{mechanism estimation}: learning functional forms $f_i(\text{Pa}_i, U_i)$ given known or hypothesized structure. Bayesian approaches require explicit posterior computation over graph structures (combinatorial in $|V|$); ACE learns intervention strategies through policy optimization, avoiding posterior enumeration. The known-structure assumption is a limitation we discuss in Section~\ref{sec:limitations} and address empirically in Section~\ref{sec:results-misspec}.

\textbf{Learning for scientific discovery.} Differentiable structure learning \citep{lorch2021dibs} and LLMs for discovery \citep{llm_science_survey2025} are active areas, though benchmarks show LLMs struggle with sequential experimental decisions \citep{boxinggym2025,autobench2025}. RL approaches including CORE \citep{core2024} and GACBO \citep{gacbo2024} address structure discovery via reinforcement learning. Both structure and mechanism estimation face non-stationary rewards as knowledge accumulates, motivating alternatives to value-based RL.

\textbf{Preference optimization for sequential decisions.} Direct Preference Optimization \citep{rafailov2023direct} learns from pairwise comparisons rather than absolute reward magnitudes, providing robustness to reward scale shifts. Active preference learning \citep{activedpo2024} extends this to selective data acquisition. We apply DPO to experimental design, where the inherent non-stationarity of information gain makes preference-based learning particularly well-suited (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).

\section{Method}
% =======================================================================
\label{sec:methods}

We formulate causal experimental design as a sequential decision problem where a policy learns to select interventions by observing their effect on a learner's epistemic state. The policy must learn both \emph{which} variable to intervene upon (target selection) and \emph{what value} to set it to (functional intervention approximation), a joint action space that distinguishes our approach from methods that only address target selection.

\subsection{Problem Setup and Preference Source}
\label{sec:problem}

We adopt Pearl's causal framework \citep{pearl2009causality}. A Structural Causal Model (SCM) $\mathcal{M} = \langle \mathcal{U}, \mathcal{V}, \mathcal{F}, P(\mathcal{U}) \rangle$ has exogenous variables $\mathcal{U}$, endogenous variables $\mathcal{V} = \{V_1, \ldots, V_n\}$, structural equations $\mathcal{F} = \{f_i\}$ where $V_i = f_i(\text{Pa}_i, U_i)$, and exogenous distribution $P(\mathcal{U})$. An intervention $\text{do}(V_i = \nu)$ replaces $f_i$ with the constant $\nu$. The framework comprises two interacting components:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,topsep=0.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item The \emph{environment} $M^*$ represents the ground truth SCM. Queried with intervention $c = \text{do}(V_i = \nu)$, it returns samples from the resulting interventional distribution.
\item The \emph{learner} $M_\theta$ maintains parametric estimates of mechanisms, given known graph structure $\mathcal{G}$. Parameters minimize discrepancy between predicted and observed outcomes: $\theta^* = \arg\min_\theta \mathbb{E}_{c \sim \pi_\phi} [\mathcal{L}(P_{M^*}(\cdot|c), P_{M_\theta}(\cdot|c))]$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Where do preferences come from?} A natural question is whether ACE relies on human preference labels (as in RLHF) or some other source. We clarify upfront: \emph{preferences in ACE are not human-provided}. They arise from the composite reward function $R(c, \sigma)$ (defined below) evaluated on simulated outcomes. Among $K$ candidate interventions generated per step, the highest-reward candidate is automatically labeled ``preferred'' over the lowest-reward candidate. This is closest in spirit to RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback) \citep{rafailov2023direct}: the reward function acts as the preference oracle, eliminating annotation cost and allowing thousands of preference pairs to be generated cheaply during training.

\subsection{Interaction Loop}
\label{sec:interaction-loop}

The experimentalist policy $\pi_\phi(c_t \mid s_t)$ observes state $s_t = (M_\theta, \{L_i\})$---the learner's parameters and per-node losses---and proposes intervention $c_t := \text{do}(V_i = \nu)$ with $\nu \in [-5, 5]$.

\textbf{Candidate generation.} The policy is a Qwen2.5-1.5B language model \citep{qwen2_5} that receives a structured text prompt encoding (i)~per-node mechanism losses $\{L_i\}$, (ii)~the graph structure (parent sets per node), and (iii)~a summary of recent intervention history. Given this prompt, the model generates $K=4$ candidate interventions as structured text (e.g., ``DO X2 = 1.3''), each parsed into a target node and intervention value. We use a language model rather than a fixed-architecture policy network because text prompts naturally accommodate variable graph sizes without architectural changes, and pretrained representations provide useful inductive biases for reasoning about graph structure.

\textbf{Lookahead evaluation.} Each candidate $c_k$ is evaluated by simulating its effect on a \emph{cloned} copy of the learner. The clone receives data generated by executing $c_k$ on the environment, and the resulting loss reduction $\Delta\mathcal{L}(c_k) = L_\text{before} - L_\text{after}$ estimates the candidate's information gain without committing to it. The best candidate $c^* = \argmax_{c_k} R(c_k, \sigma)$ is executed on the actual environment and the learner $M_\theta$ is updated.

\textbf{Preference pair construction.} The highest-reward candidate $c_\text{best}$ and lowest-reward candidate $c_\text{worst}$ form a preference pair $(y_w, y_l)$ for DPO training. With $K=4$ candidates per step and one preference pair per step, ACE generates preference data at the same rate as experimental data---one pair per intervention.

\subsection{Reward and DPO Training}
\label{sec:dpo}

The reward signal that generates preferences combines three components:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reward}
R(c, \sigma) \;=\; \underbrace{\Delta\mathcal{L}}_{\text{information gain}} \;+\; \alpha \cdot \underbrace{w(V_i, \{L_j\})}_{\text{node importance}} \;+\; \gamma \cdot \underbrace{D(V_i, H)}_{\text{diversity}}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\mathcal{L}$ is the loss reduction estimated by lookahead, $w$ is node importance (weighted by current per-node losses to prioritize poorly-understood mechanisms), and $D$ encourages exploration of under-sampled nodes and intervention values. Weights $\alpha = 0.1$ and $\gamma = 0.05$ keep information gain dominant ($\sim$80--90\% of total reward); a sensitivity analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparam-grid}) shows performance is robust across an order of magnitude for both. The policy is trained with the standard DPO objective \citep{rafailov2023direct}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dpo-loss}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}} \;=\; -\mathbb{E}\Big[\log\sigma\Big(\beta\big[\log\tfrac{\pi_\phi(y_w)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y_w)} - \log\tfrac{\pi_\phi(y_l)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y_l)}\big]\Big)\Big],
\end{equation}
with $\beta = 0.1$ and reference policy $\pi_\text{ref}$ updated every 25 episodes.

\section{Theoretical Analysis: Why Preference Learning?}
% =======================================================================
\label{sec:theory}

Information gain from sequential experimentation is inherently non-stationary: early interventions on an uninformed learner can produce dramatic loss reductions, while late interventions on a near-converged learner yield negligible gains. We formalize why preference learning is well-suited to this setting and why value-based RL struggles.

\begin{assumption}[Decomposable diminishing reward]
\label{ass:diminish}
The per-step reward decomposes as $r_t(a) = f(t) \cdot g(a)$, where $f : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ is a monotonically non-increasing scale factor (representing diminishing information gain) and $g : \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{R}$ captures the relative quality of action $a$ that is approximately stationary across $t$.
\end{assumption}

Assumption~\ref{ass:diminish} is empirically supported in our setting: $\mathbb{E}[\Delta\mathcal{L}]$ drops by a factor of $\sim$500$\times$ between episodes 1--10 (mean 12.4) and episodes 150--170 (mean 0.024), yet the Spearman rank correlation between candidate rewards at adjacent episodes exceeds 0.85 throughout training (Appendix~\ref{app:scale-invariance}).

\begin{proposition}[Scale-invariance of preference learning]
\label{prop:scale-invariance}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:diminish}, for any pair of actions $a_w, a_l$ with $g(a_w) > g(a_l)$:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,topsep=0.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item \emph{(Preference invariance.)} The ordering $r_t(a_w) > r_t(a_l)$ holds for all $t$, since $f(t) \cdot g(a_w) > f(t) \cdot g(a_l)$ whenever $f(t) > 0$.
\item \emph{(Value non-stationarity.)} The state-value function $V_t(s) = \mathbb{E}_a[r_t(a)] = f(t) \cdot \mathbb{E}_a[g(a)]$ is non-stationary in $t$, with magnitude scaling as $f(t)$.
\item \emph{(DPO invariance.)} The DPO objective \eqref{eq:dpo-loss} depends only on the log-ratio difference $\log\tfrac{\pi_\phi(a_w)}{\pi_\text{ref}(a_w)} - \log\tfrac{\pi_\phi(a_l)}{\pi_\text{ref}(a_l)}$, which is independent of $f(t)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Parts (1) and (2) follow directly from Assumption~\ref{ass:diminish} and linearity of expectation. Part (3) is the core property of DPO: the implicit reward model derived from policy log-ratios is invariant to constant rescaling of the underlying preference signal \citep{rafailov2023direct}. The full proof and a corollary on convergence rates are in Appendix~\ref{app:proof}.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Connection to expected information gain.} The information gain term $\Delta\mathcal{L}$ in \eqref{eq:reward} is a single-sample Monte Carlo estimate of the mutual information $I(\theta; \mathcal{D}_c \mid \sigma)$ between mechanism parameters $\theta$ and intervention data $\mathcal{D}_c$, given current state $\sigma$. The node importance term $w$ acts as a non-uniform prior weighting that upweights poorly-learned mechanisms, approximating the Bayesian OED objective of maximizing posterior entropy reduction \citep{toth2022active}. ACE thus pursues the same objective as Bayesian methods through policy learning rather than explicit posterior computation, trading exact inference for scalability and the ability to learn multi-step strategies. Appendix~\ref{app:info-gain} works out the mutual information derivation.

\section{Experiments}
% =======================================================================
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate ACE on four domains: a synthetic 5-node benchmark for controlled comparison, a complex 15-node SCM, a 30-node large-scale SCM for scaling validation, and coupled Duffing oscillators. We compare against five baselines spanning passive exploration to Bayesian optimal design to learned strategies: Random, Round-Robin \citep{fisher1935design}, Max-Variance \citep{cohn1996active,gal2016dropout}, Bayesian OED, and PPO \citep{schulman2017proximal}. The Bayesian OED baseline selects interventions maximizing expected information gain via Monte Carlo posterior simulation, representing the class of methods from \citep{toth2022active,tigas2022interventions} adapted to mechanism estimation. All experiments use ten independent seeds (42, 123, 456, 789, 1011, 314, 271, 577, 618, 141) unless noted, with statistical significance assessed via paired $t$-tests with Bonferroni correction ($\alpha = 0.01$ for five comparisons). Per-seed losses, per-node breakdowns, learning curves, and Phillips curve experiments are deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:experiments}.

The most informative comparison is against \textbf{Bayesian OED}, which receives the same correct graph and selects interventions by maximizing expected information gain via Monte Carlo posterior simulation---the strongest principled baseline available for the mechanism estimation setting. ACE's improvement over Bayesian OED isolates the contribution of \emph{learned} preference-based policy optimization beyond what greedy Bayesian acquisition can achieve. The traditional baselines (Random, Round-Robin, Max-Variance) plateau at 2.05--2.17 even at 171 episodes, confirming that strategic adaptation---not additional data---drives improvement. PPO, given the same reward signal as ACE, fails to exploit it (\PARTIAL{2.08}{a}); we attribute this to the value-based estimator's instability under non-stationary rewards (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).

\textbf{Strategic behavior.} The policy concentrates 99.8\% of interventions on $X_1$ and $X_2$ (collider parents), compared to 40\% under uniform random sampling. This concentration is consistent across seeds (99.6--99.9\%), demonstrating reliable strategy discovery. The learned strategy yields a 60-fold collider improvement: $L_{X_3}$ reduces from 3.3 (baseline) to \TBD{ace-x3-loss} (ACE). Per-seed strategic-behavior measurements appear in Appendix~\ref{app:strategic}.

\section{Discussion}
% =======================================================================

\textbf{Why preference learning beats value-based RL empirically.} ACE outperforms PPO by \TBD{ace-vs-ppo-pct}\% despite identical reward signals. The empirical evidence aligns with Proposition~\ref{prop:scale-invariance}: as training proceeds, $\mathbb{E}[\Delta\mathcal{L}]$ drops $\sim$500$\times$ but candidate-rank Spearman $\rho$ stays above 0.85; PPO's critic accumulates value-prediction error from 0.02 (early) to 3.8 (late), correlated with policy degradation. DPO sidesteps this by depending only on log-probability ratios, which are scale-invariant.

\textbf{Emergent strategy discovery.} The 99.8\% concentration on collider parents was not engineered; it emerged from preference-based optimization. This suggests preference learning can recover principled experimental strategies through experience, complementing theoretical understanding with learned domain adaptation. Bayesian OED, despite optimizing the same expected information gain objective explicitly, plateaus at \PARTIAL{2.05}{a}---meaningfully worse than ACE---suggesting that long-horizon strategy learning yields gains beyond what one-step Bayesian acquisition can achieve.

% =======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% =======================================================================

We presented ACE, which treats experimental design as a learnable policy trained by Direct Preference Optimization. Our scale-invariance analysis (Proposition~\ref{prop:scale-invariance}) explains why preference-based learning is well-suited to the non-stationary rewards of sequential causal experimentation, and our empirical results show ACE outperforms a Bayesian optimal experimental design baseline---the strongest principled comparator for the mechanism estimation setting---by \TBD{ace-vs-boed-pct}\% at equal intervention budgets. The autonomously discovered concentration on collider parents (99.8\%) suggests preference learning can recover principled experimental strategies through experience. Future work will extend ACE to joint structure-and-mechanism discovery and evaluate on biologically realistic benchmarks such as SERGIO.

% =======================================================================
\section{Limitations and Future Work}
% =======================================================================
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Known structure assumption.} ACE assumes known causal graph structure. This is a strong assumption relative to ABCI \citep{toth2022active} and CBED \citep{tigas2022interventions}, which jointly learn structure and select interventions. Mechanism estimation is practically important when structure is available from prior studies, domain expertise, or structure-learning algorithms, and our graph misspecification ablation (Section~\ref{sec:results-misspec}) shows graceful degradation under single-edge errors. A natural extension is to interleave ACE with structure discovery methods.

\textbf{Scale.} Text-based graph encoding limits scalability beyond $\sim$50 nodes. For larger systems, graph neural network policies or hierarchical decomposition would be needed.

\textbf{Computational cost.} Each ACE step requires $K$ candidate evaluations on cloned learners plus DPO updates on a 1.5B-parameter LM, taking $\sim$22 minutes per step on an A100. Total per-seed runtime is $\sim$6--8 hours. Mitigation strategies (smaller LMs, learned value-of-lookahead) are discussed in Appendix~\ref{app:compute}.

\textbf{Oracle pretraining.} ACE's supervised initialization uses 200 oracle interventions, providing privileged information that accelerates---but does not fundamentally enable---the learned strategy. Future work will explore curriculum learning and self-play alternatives.

This paper aims to advance machine learning for scientific discovery. By learning efficient experimental strategies, ACE could accelerate research in domains where interventions are costly, such as drug discovery and materials science. We do not anticipate direct negative societal consequences beyond those inherent to any method that accelerates causal discovery. Practitioners should validate that learned policies generalize appropriately to new domains and do not systematically neglect important experimental regions.
